**Title:** How Foreign Names Become Chinese: The Hidden System Behind 特朗普, 牛津, and 安娜

**Subtitle:** Chinese renderings of foreign names look arbitrary. They aren't. Here's the system translators actually use.

---

If you've ever seen *Trump* written as 特朗普, *Oxford* as 牛津, or *Anna* as 安娜, you might assume someone just picked characters that sounded vaguely right. In fact, professional translation into Chinese follows a surprisingly systematic set of rules. Once you see the system, the names stop looking random.

## Two ways a name crosses into Chinese

There are really only two mechanisms:

1. **Transliteration** — matching the *sound*. This is what happens to the vast majority of foreign personal names. 特朗普 (*Tè-lǎng-pǔ*) doesn't mean anything; it just approximates the sound of "Trump."
2. **Semantic translation** — matching the *meaning*. This is rarer and mostly shows up in place names. 牛津 for Oxford is literally "ox" (牛) + "ford" (津, an old word for a river crossing).

Most names you'll meet are pure transliteration. But the interesting part is *how* the sound-matching is standardized.

## The transliteration tables

Chinese has no small phonetic alphabet like Japanese katakana. What it has instead is a set of **transliteration tables (译音表)**, maintained by Xinhua News Agency's Proper Names & Translation Service (新华社译名室). These tables are the backbone of professional name translation, and they have a few important properties.

**There is one table per source language.** Separate tables exist for English, French, German, Russian, Italian, and so on. This is necessary because the same letters are pronounced differently across languages. *Charles* becomes 查尔斯 (*Chá-ěr-sī*) from English, but 夏尔 (*Xià-ěr*) from French. A single universal table simply can't work — the rules have to know which language the name came from.

**Each table maps a sound to one recommended character.** Mandarin is full of homophones: the syllable *lì* alone can be written 丽, 莉, 利, 力, 立, and more. Left to chance, every translator would pick a different character and the same name would appear a dozen different ways. The tables fix one preferred character per sound, often with separate columns for word-initial vs. word-final position. The whole point is **consistency**: so that one person's name is written the same way in every newspaper, book, and database.

## Why 安娜 and not 安那

The tables also encode a subtle convention: **characters carry a hint of gender.** Female given names lean on soft, graceful characters — 娜, 丽, 莉, 娅, 琳. So *Anna* is 安娜 (with the feminine 娜), not 安那. Male names lean on neutral or strong ones — 德, 克, 夫, 尔, 罗 — which is why *Robert* is 罗伯特. Two characters can sound identical and still be "wrong" if they signal the wrong gender.

## The characters are sound, not meaning

A key principle: in systematic name transliteration, the characters are chosen as **phonetic markers**, not for what they mean. The tables deliberately avoid rare, unpleasant, or strongly meaningful characters, precisely so a reader treats the name as *sound* rather than reading meaning into it.

The famous exceptions are brands, where sound *and* meaning are courted at once — Coca-Cola became 可口可乐 (*Kěkǒu kělè*, roughly "tasty and joyful"). But that's a marketing luxury. An ordinary person's name doesn't chase meaning; it just needs to be phonetically accurate and consistently spelled.

## When the rules don't apply: 约定俗成

Not every name follows the tables, because some renderings were fixed long before the tables existed. The Chinese for *John* is 约翰 (*Yuēhàn*) — which sounds nothing like English "John," because it came in through the Latin/Hebrew *Yohanan*. *George* is 乔治. These are cases of 约定俗成 — "established by convention." Once a rendering is widely used, it stays, even if it breaks the phonetic rules. Translators are expected to *know* these established forms rather than re-derive them.

Place names often mix the two mechanisms for the same reason. *Cambridge* is 剑桥, where 剑 transcribes "Cam" and 桥 translates "-bridge." *Oxford* is the fully semantic 牛津. There's no single rule — there's a standard rendering you're expected to look up.

## The little dot: ·

One last detail translators notice immediately: foreign full names use a middle dot, ·, to separate the parts. *David Beckham* is 大卫·贝克汉姆. The dot tells the reader "this is one foreign name with multiple parts," not two separate Chinese words.

## Why this matters

For a translator or editor, the goal isn't creativity — it's **getting the established, standardized rendering right**, every time, so your text matches everyone else's. That means two tasks: looking up names that already have a fixed Chinese form, and correctly transliterating new ones by source language.

The next time you see 特朗普 or 牛津, you'll know it wasn't a guess. It was a system.
